Today I will present MindZero: Learning Online Mental Reasoning With Zero Annotations. We study the problem called online mental reasoning, that is, how an AI assistant can infer a person's goal or belief while the interaction is unfolding, and then use that estimate for assistance.

01 / Online Mental Reasoning

Infer mental states from a partial behavior stream

At every time step, the assistant maintains mental-state hypotheses over latent human goals.

  • Uncertainty robust uncertainty over multiple hypotheses
  • Efficiency fast inference for real-time assistance
  • Zero annotations learning with zero ground-truth annotations
The figure shows an example from our real results. A human is acting in an environment, but the assistant does not observe the human's goal or belief. At each time step, it sees only the state and action. The assistant must maintain mental-state hypotheses over latent human goals and update them online. Early in a trajectory, several goals may explain the same behavior. Later, the distribution should become sharper. This creates the three challenges: robust uncertainty over multiple hypotheses, fast inference for real-time assistance, and learning with zero ground-truth annotations.

02 / Bayesian Theory of Mind

A standard target, but expensive to run online

P(mt | s1:t, a1:t) ∝ P(a1:t | mt, s1:t) · P(mt)

Posterior the mental-state distribution on the left side

Likelihood how likely the observed actions are under this state

Prior whether the mental state itself is plausible

Model-based ToM (e.g. AutoToM) estimates it through Bayesian networks, but each edge may require an LLM call, which is slow and expensive.

A standard formulation is Bayesian Theory of Mind. In the equation, the left side is the posterior, or the mental-state distribution. It is proportional to an action likelihood times a prior. The likelihood asks how likely the observed actions are under this state. The prior asks whether the mental state itself is plausible. This gives us a standard target, and model-based ToM methods such as AutoToM can estimate it through Bayesian networks. But at every time step, each edge in the Bayesian network may require an LLM call, which makes online inference slow and expensive.

03 / Self-Supervised Reinforcement Learning

Amortize model-based ToM into one forward pass

Model-based reasoning can be used as the training signal for amortization.

  • Proposal A full particle set of candidate mental states
  • Scoring A planner or frozen LLM scorer checks action likelihood
  • Optimization Reinforcement learning for non-differentiable scoring

At test time, the model can produce hypotheses in a single pass.

However, model-based reasoning provides us a strong scoring function, which can be used as the training signal to amortizes multimodal language models. During training, the model proposes a full particle set of candidate mental states. A planner or frozen LLM scorer checks their action likelihood. This provides a self-supervised signal, which means we don't need mental state annotations at all. Because this scoring is non-differentiable, we use reinforcement learning to train the model. At test time, the model can produce hypotheses in a single pass.

04 / Objective

ELBO as the reward

ELBO encourages hypotheses that explain actions and keep uncertainty.

  • Likelihood high action likelihood
  • Prior prior plausibility
  • Entropy discourages early collapse
Here is the reward in more detail. It uses ELBO as the reward to encourage hypotheses that explain actions and keep uncertainty when needed. Specifically, one part rewards high action likelihood, another captures prior plausibility, and the entropy term discourages early collapse when evidence is still ambiguous.

05 / Setup

Domains and tasks for evaluation

Domains

  • GridWorld: visual map input
  • Household: text-converted scenarios

Tasks

  • Story-based Theory-of-Mind question answering
  • Proactive assistance
Moving to evaluation, we use two domains shown here. In GridWorld, the model receives a visual map input and reasons multimodally. In Household, we follow prior work and convert multimodal task descriptions into text. In both domains, we evaluate both story-based Theory-of-Mind question answering and proactive assistance. Question answering checks inference at one point, while proactive assistance is harder, because the agent must update multiple hypotheses throughout the trajectory.

06 / Question Answering

MindZero improves story-based QA accuracy

MindZero improves significantly over the pretrained checkpoint and is competitive with strong commercial models using much less computation.

First, on question answering, pretrained models alone are even below chance. After MindZero training, performance improves significantly over the pretrained checkpoint and is competitive with strong commercial-model baselines, while using much less computation.

07 / Proactive Assistance

Proactive assistance tests online inference

MindZero obtains the best speedup efficiently collaborating with both simulated and real humans.

Next, on proactive assistance, pretrained models perform poorly, while MindZero obtains the best speedup efficiently among the tested baselines when collaborating with both simulated human agents and real participants.

08 / Analysis

Mode seeking does not become mode collapse

One concern is the diversity of the hypotheses. The ELBO uses a reverse-KL-style objective, which can be mode seeking. But in online assistance, some reduction in diversity is expected. Early in the task, the model should keep several hypotheses alive. Later, probability should concentrate on the likely goals. The left figure shows this sharpening as task progress increases. The right table shows that diversity depends on prior design that encourages diversity, structured output for multiple hypotheses, and the entropy bonus. All three contribute to performance.

09 / Takeaways

Mental reasoning can be learned with self-supervision

Problem Online mental reasoning with uncertainty, efficiency, and zero annotations

Method Online mental reasoning can be learned with self-supervision using RL

Result Efficient single-pass inference at test time and strong results across domains and tasks

To summarize, MindZero addresses online mental reasoning under three constraints: uncertainty, efficiency, and zero annotations. Our work proves that online mental reasoning can be learned with self-supervision using RL, and the trained model can efficiently perform single-pass inference at test time, achieving strong results across domains and tasks.
